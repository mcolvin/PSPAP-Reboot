Objectives weighting: a swing weighting exercise

Background

The purpose of this post is to elicit input on how various metrics may be weighted when valuing the outcomes of a montitoring design with respect to the fundamental objectives. In order to do so, we ask that you fill out a swing weighting form (excel workbook linked in step 1 below). The PSPAP 2.0 team will not be making decisions based on this information. Instead, the information will be summarized and used to calculate the expected utility (i.e., what is the value of alternative monitoring designs to achieve objectives) for decision maker comparison.

In order to understand swing weighting and how it applies to some of the particular objectives and metrics, we are also including links to a tutorial and examples.

It may take 10 to 20 minutes to work through this exercise. We recognize this is a tedious task however, it provides a transparent, robust, and defensible approach to quantifying how objectives may be weighted. Thank you all for your participation!

For those looking for a more in depth understanding of how and why we are using swing weighting in PSPAP 2.0, please watch the 11 minute swing weighting YouTube tutorial Here.

Instructions

  1. Be sure you can open, edit, and save the swing weighting exercise (it’s an excel file with 5 sheets): Download Exercise Spreadsheet Here
  2. Fill in your Name and Agency information on the worksheet titled “Meta”
  3. On the Abundance worksheet do the following: (Note, these instructions are also on the worksheet.)
    1. See the 5 minute YouTube video demonstrating how to fill in the abundance table: Abundance example
    2. Rank each of the scenarios (i.e. rows). The worst case scenario is already filled in for you with a rank of 3 and a value of 0.
    3. Identify your best scenario from the remaining rows and give that a rank of 1.
    4. Now rank the remaining scenarios in your order of importance.
    5. Assign the scenario that you ranked 1 with a value of 100.
    6. Assign a value, from 0 to 100, to the remaining scenarios (0 is worst and 100 is the best), making sure that your values increase with rank. For example a scenario with a rank of 2 should have a higher value than a scenario with a rank of 3. (Values do not need to sum to 100.)
  4. On the Trend worksheet do the following: (Note, these instructions are also on the worksheet.)
    1. See the 4 minute YouTube video demonstrating how to fill in the trend table Trend example
    2. Rank each of the scenarios (i.e. rows). The worst case scenario is already filled in for you with a rank of 3 and a value of 0.
    3. Identify your best scenario from the remaining rows and give that a rank of 1.
    4. Now rank the remaining scenarios in your order of importance.
    5. Assign the scenario that you ranked 1 with a value of 100.
    6. Assign a value, from 0 to 100, to the remaining scenarios (0 is worst and 100 is the best), making sure that your values increase with rank. For example a scenario with a rank of 2 should have a higher value than a scenario with a rank of 3. (Values do not need to sum to 100.)
  5. On the Fundamental objectives worksheet do the following: (Note, these instructions are also on the worksheet.)
    1. Rank each of the scenarios (i.e. rows). The worst case scenario is already filled in for you with a rank of 5 and a value of 0.
    2. Identify your best scenario from the remaining rows and give that a rank of 1.
    3. Now rank the remaining scenarios in your order of importance.
    4. Assign the scenario that you ranked 1 with a value of 100.
    5. Assign a value, from 0 to 100, to the remaining scenarios (0 is worst and 100 is the best), making sure that your values increase with rank. For example a scenario with a rank of 2 should have a higher value than a scenario with a rank of 3. (Values do not need to sum to 100.)
  6. Once you have completed filling in all 5 sheets of the swing weighting exercise, email the filled in form to: s.reynolds@msstate.edu
